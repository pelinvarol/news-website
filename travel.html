India’s elephant-friendly tea garden

Although elephants do not eat tea leaves, they often pass through the fields during their long migrations, encountering hostile humans along the way. During the last 75 years, the Asian elephant population in India has declined by 50%, and in 2018 an estimated 64 people died as a result of human-elephant conflict. In the Indian state of Assam, tea farmer Tenzing Bodosa has found his own way of creating a more symbiotic human-elephant relationship. Bodosa’s three-hectare elephant-friendly tea garden forgoes the dangerous drainage ditches and electric fences used by other growers in the area, allowing migrating elephants to move through the farm without the threat of injury.




The man who mapped nation by hand, known as 'Indiana Jones'

Telfor Bedeau has kayaked around his Caribbean island home, walked nearly every inch of it and climbed its highest peak 217 times. Known as "the Indiana Jones of Grenada", Bedeau is something of a nationwide celebrity. He has walked, rowed and mapped nearly every nook and cranny of Grenada's rugged terrain, covering 16,093km and climbing its highest peak, Mount Saint Catherine, a staggering 217 times. Bedeau has only lived away from the island once, in 1958, when he travelled to England on an ocean liner after being inspired by the Dickens and Shakespeare his grandmother encouraged him to read as a child. Bedeau started keeping a record of his journeys in 1962.




She's the first female tour guide in Afghanistan

That has been the story of Fatima, the only woman working as a tour guide in Afghanistan. The 22-year-old grew up leading sheep through the countryside, and now she leads tourists through the streets of Herat, the third-largest city in Afghanistan. The youngest of eight children, Fatima is the only one of her siblings to be unmarried and to have gotten an education. Fatima's new career caused some friction in her traditional family at a time when her siblings were already challenging their father's more conservative views. She says she hopes to open a school to train tour guides. "I am the first lady in Afghanistan to guide people," she says, "but I do not want to be the last.""
